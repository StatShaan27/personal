Foundational axioms of depth statistics

This section introduces the foundational axioms of depth statistics. A depth function assigns to a point \(x\) a numerical value measuring how central it is relative to a distribution or data cloud.

The framework here is conceptual rather than computational. The goal is to formalize what counts as a legitimate notion of ‚Äúcentrality,‚Äù independent of any particular construction.

Concretely, the authors are doing three things:

  • Giving a general abstract definition of depth
  • Stating axioms (postulates) such a function must satisfy
  • Explaining how these axioms encode geometric, probabilistic, and robustness properties

Mathematical setting

Space and probability structure

  • State space: \(E\) is a Banach space, i.e. a complete normed vector space.
    • \(\mathbb{R}^d\) with the Euclidean norm
    • Function spaces such as \(L^p\)
  • Sigma-algebra: \(\mathcal{B}\) is the Borel œÉ-algebra on \(E\), generated by all open sets.
  • Distributions: \(\mathcal{P}\) is a class of probability measures on \((E,\mathcal{B})\).
    • Often all probability measures
    • Sometimes restricted (e.g. finite moments)
  • Random element: \(X\) is an \(E\)-valued random variable with distribution \(P \in \mathcal{P}\).

Definition: depth function

A depth function is a mapping

\[ D : E \times \mathcal{P} \to [0,1], \qquad (x,P) \mapsto D(x \mid P). \]

Interpretation:

  • \(D(x \mid P)\) measures how central the point \(x\) is relative to \(P\)
  • Larger values indicate greater centrality
  • The range \([0,1]\) is a normalization convention

Postulates D1‚ÄìD5

These are axioms, not derived properties. A function that violates them is not considered a valid depth within this framework.

D1. Translation invariance

Statement.

\[ D(x + b \mid X + b) = D(x \mid X), \quad \forall b \in E. \]

Meaning. Shifting both the point and the distribution by the same vector leaves depth unchanged.

Rationale. Depth depends on relative position, not absolute coordinates.

D2. Linear invariance

Statement.

\[ D(Ax \mid AX) = D(x \mid X) \]

for every bijective linear transformation \(A : E \to E\).

Meaning. Depth is invariant under changes of units, rotations, reflections, and shearings.

Why bijectivity matters. Non-invertible maps destroy information and cannot preserve centrality.

D3. Null at infinity

Statement.

\[ \lim_{|x|\to\infty} D(x \mid X) = 0. \]

Meaning. Points arbitrarily far from the data cloud have negligible depth.

Role. Excludes pathological definitions assigning large depth far away from the data.

D4. Monotonicity on rays

Statement. If \(x^*\) is a point of maximal depth, then for any unit vector \(r\),

\[ D(x^* + \alpha r \mid X) \quad \text{decreases as } \alpha > 0 \text{ increases}. \]

Meaning. Moving away from a deepest point in any direction reduces centrality.

Role. Prevents oscillatory or non-radial depth behavior.

D5. Upper semicontinuity

Statement. For every \(\alpha \in (0,1]\),

\[ \{x \in E : D(x \mid X) \ge \alpha\} \quad \text{is closed}. \]

Consequences.

  • Ensures existence of maximizers
  • Guarantees stability under small perturbations

Upper semicontinuity rules out sudden upward jumps of depth.

Consequences of D1‚ÄìD4

  • D1 and D2 imply affine invariance
  • D3 and D4 imply that upper level sets are bounded and star-shaped around deepest points

Star-shaped sets

A set \(S\) is star-shaped about \(x^*\) if

\[ x \in S \Rightarrow [x^*,x] \subset S. \]

This guarantees that central regions are solid, connected regions rather than disconnected shells.

Proposition 2.1

Statement. If \(X\) is centrally symmetric about \(x^*\), then any depth function satisfying D1‚ÄìD5 is maximized at \(x^*\).

Central symmetry

\[ X - x^* \;\overset{d}{=}\; x^* - X. \]

This proposition ensures that depth correctly identifies the center under symmetry.

Strengthened and weakened axioms

D4con (quasiconcavity)

Upper level sets of depth are convex.

Depths satisfying this condition are called convex depths. Convexity implies star-shapedness, so D4con is stronger than D4.

D2iso (isometric invariance)

\[ D(Ax \mid AX) = D(x \mid X) \]

only for isometries, i.e. distance-preserving maps.

This weakens affine invariance to preserve scale and variance structure.

D2sca (scale invariance)

\[ D(\lambda x \mid \lambda X) = D(x \mid X), \quad \forall \lambda > 0. \]

Depth should not depend on the units of measurement.

2.2.2 Central Regions and Outliers

Restated goal of the section

This section explains how a depth function induces:

  • Central regions (multivariate analogues of quantiles)
  • A notion of outlyingness
  • An ordering of points from ‚Äúmost central‚Äù to ‚Äúmost extreme‚Äù

This forms the bridge between abstract depth axioms and practical data analysis.

Step 1: Level sets of a depth function

Given objects

  • \(P \in \mathcal P\): a probability distribution on \(E\)
  • \(D(\cdot \mid P)\): a depth function satisfying D1‚ÄìD5
  • \(\alpha \in [0,1]\)

Definition: \(\alpha\)-central region

\[ D_\alpha(P) \;:=\; \{x \in E : D(x \mid P) \ge \alpha\}. \]

These are also called depth-trimmed regions or central regions.

Formal properties

From axioms D3‚ÄìD5 and possibly D4con:

  • Closed (D5)
  • Bounded (D3)
  • Star-shaped (D4)
  • Convex if D4con holds

Nested structure

If \(0 \le \alpha_1 < \alpha_2 \le 1\), then

\[ D_{\alpha_2}(P) \subseteq D_{\alpha_1}(P). \]

Higher depth corresponds to a stricter inclusion criterion.

Step 2: The deepest point(s)

Define

\[ \alpha_{\max} := \sup_{x \in E} D(x \mid P). \]

Then \(D_{\alpha_{\max}}(P)\) is the set of deepest points.

  • If unique, it is a multivariate median
  • Otherwise, it forms a deepest region

Step 3: Affine equivariance of central regions

From D1 and D2, if \(A\) is affine and bijective, then

\[ D_\alpha(AP) = A D_\alpha(P). \]

Central regions transform exactly like the data, preserving geometry such as location, spread, and shape.

Step 4: From regions to depth

Assumption

We are given a nested family of sets \(\{C_\alpha(P)\}_{\alpha \in [0,1]}\), each of which is convex, bounded, closed, and nested in \(\alpha\).

Definition (Equation 2.1)

\[ D(z \mid P) \;:=\; \sup\{\alpha : z \in C_\alpha(P)\}. \tag{2.1} \]

Formal justification

  • Nestedness ensures the supremum is well defined
  • Closedness implies upper semicontinuity
  • Convexity yields quasiconcavity

This construction automatically satisfies D5 and D4con.

Step 5: Depth ordering and outlyingness

Center‚Äìoutward ordering

\[ D(x \mid X) > D(y \mid X) \;\Rightarrow\; x \text{ is more central than } y. \]

Depth induces an ordering from the center to the periphery.

Step 6: Outlyingness function

Definition

\[ \mathrm{Out}(z \mid X) \;:=\; \frac{1}{D(z \mid X)} - 1. \]

Properties

  • If \(z\) is deepest: \(D=1 \Rightarrow \mathrm{Out}=0\)
  • If \(D \downarrow 0\): \(\mathrm{Out} \uparrow \infty\)

The transformation converts ‚Äúlarge depth‚Äù into ‚Äúsmall outlyingness.‚Äù

\[ D(z \mid X) = \frac{1}{1 + \mathrm{Out}(z \mid X)}. \]

Step 7: Outliers via central regions

If

\[ z \notin D_\alpha(X), \]

then

\[ \mathrm{Out}(z \mid X) > \frac{1}{\alpha} - 1. \]

This yields a distribution-free notion of multivariate outliers.

2.2.3 Depth Lifts, Stochastic Orderings, and Metrics

Goal of the section

Depth induces:

  • A partial ordering on distributions
  • A notion of dispersion
  • A distance (semi-metric) between distributions

Step 1: Normalization

Assume

\[ \alpha_{\max} = 1. \]

This is a harmless rescaling of depth.

Step 2: Definition of the depth lift

\[ \widehat D(P) \;:=\; \{(\alpha,x) \in [0,1] \times E : x \in D_\alpha(P)\}. \tag{2.2} \]

This stacks all central regions along the \(\alpha\)-axis into a single geometric object.

Step 3: Dispersion ordering of distributions

\[ P \preceq_D Q \quad \text{if} \quad \widehat D(P) \subseteq \widehat D(Q). \tag{2.3} \]

Equivalently,

\[ D_\alpha(P) \subseteq D_\alpha(Q) \quad \text{for all } \alpha. \]

This means that \(Q\) is more dispersed than \(P\).

Step 4: Partial order vs preorder

  • If the family \(\{D_\alpha(P)\}\) uniquely determines \(P\), then \(\preceq_D\) is a partial order
  • Otherwise, it is only a preorder

Step 5: Depth-induced distance

\[ \delta_D(P,Q) \;:=\; \delta_H(\widehat D(P), \widehat D(Q)), \tag{2.4} \]

where \(\delta_H\) is the Hausdorff distance.

Hausdorff distance

\[ \delta_H(C_1,C_2) = \inf\{\varepsilon>0 : C_1 \subseteq C_2^\varepsilon \text{ and } C_2 \subseteq C_1^\varepsilon \}. \]

Two distributions are close if their entire depth geometry is close.

2.3 Multivariate Depth Functions

What this section is really about

Up to this point, depth has been defined axiomatically. Section 2.3 addresses the natural next question:

Which concrete depth functions actually exist, and how do they relate to the geometry of distributions?

The central insight is that depth functions are not universal. Their behavior is tightly coupled to distributional shape, especially ellipticity, and to the invariance structure they satisfy.

Step 1: Why ellipticity matters

Definition

A random vector \(X \in \mathbb{R}^d\) has a spherical distribution if

\[ AX \stackrel{d}{=} X \quad \text{for every orthogonal matrix } A. \]

It has an elliptical distribution if

\[ X = \mu + B Y, \]

where:

  • \(Y\) is spherical
  • \(B\) is a nonsingular \(d \times d\) matrix
  • \(\mu \in \mathbb{R}^d\)

This class includes multivariate normal, multivariate \(t\), and many heavy-tailed models.

Geometric meaning

  • Spherical distributions have spherical level sets
  • Elliptical distributions have ellipsoidal level sets

This geometry determines what any affine invariant depth can detect.

Step 2: Depth under ellipticity

Assume:

  • \(X\) is elliptically distributed
  • \(D(\cdot \mid P)\) satisfies affine invariance (D2)

Key consequence

All depth contours \(D_\alpha(P)\) are ellipsoids with the same shape matrix as the distribution.

Equivalently, depth reduces to a radial function:

\[ D(x \mid P) = f\!\left((x-\mu)'\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu)\right), \]

for some decreasing function \(f\).

Why this is forced

  • Elliptical distributions are affine images of spherical ones
  • Affine invariance forces depth contours to transform accordingly
  • Spherical symmetry forces contours to be spheres
  • Affine images of spheres are ellipsoids

No additional assumptions are required.

Crucial implication

For elliptically distributed data, all affine invariant depth functions induce the same ordering.

They may differ numerically, but they rank points identically. In particular, Tukey depth, simplicial depth, Oja depth, and projection depth are equivalent as orderings under ellipticity.

Step 3: Consequences for inference

If \(P\) is elliptically symmetric and unimodal:

  • All affine invariant depths identify the same center \(\mu\)
  • All central regions have the same shape
  • All depths reduce to monotone functions of Mahalanobis distance

This explains why classical Gaussian methods perform well under ellipticity and why depth methods differ meaningfully only outside this regime.

Step 4: Beyond ellipticity

For non-elliptical distributions:

  • Different depths produce different central regions
  • They respond differently to skewness and multimodality
  • They may or may not uniquely determine the distribution

Depth functions then differ in robustness, sensitivity to asymmetry, geometry, and computational feasibility.

Step 5: Three main construction principles

The text identifies three fundamental ways to construct multivariate depths.

(I) Distance-based depths

Core idea: outlyingness is distance from a suitable center.

\[ D(x) = g\!\left(\mathrm{dist}(x,\text{center})\right). \]

Centers may be means, medians, or deepest points; distances may be Euclidean, Mahalanobis, or robust.

These depths are intuitive and computationally simple, but often center-dependent and sensitive to shape misspecification.

(II) Weighted-mean depths

Core idea: central points balance the data.

\[ D(x) = \left\lVert \sum_i w_i(x)(x_i - x) \right\rVert^{-1}. \]

This class includes spatial depth and \(L_1\)-type constructions. They are smooth and robust but may lack strong geometric guarantees or affine invariance.

(III) Simplicial and halfspace depths

Core idea: central points participate in many balanced geometric configurations.

  • Halfspace (Tukey) depth
  • Simplicial depth
  • Oja depth

These depths have strong geometric meaning and robustness, but are computationally expensive and have more complex asymptotics.

Step 6: No universally optimal depth

No depth function satisfies all desirable properties simultaneously. Trade-offs are unavoidable:

  • Robustness versus computability
  • Smoothness versus exact geometry
  • Affine invariance versus sensitivity to shape

Depth theory is therefore a theory of principled choices rather than optimization.

\(L_2\)-Depth and Axiom D1 (Translation Invariance)

Axiom D1: Formal statement

Let \(X\) be an \(\mathbb{R}^d\)-valued random vector with distribution \(P\). Let \(D(\cdot \mid X)\) be a statistical depth function.

Axiom D1 (Translation Invariance). For every \(z \in \mathbb{R}^d\) and every translation vector \(b \in \mathbb{R}^d\),

\[ D(z \mid X) = D(z + b \mid X + b), \]

where \(X+b\) denotes the random vector defined by

\[ X+b := X + b \quad \text{almost surely}. \]

Meaning of Axiom D1

Translation invariance requires that depth depends only on relative position, not on absolute location. If the entire data cloud and the point under inspection are shifted by the same vector, their geometric relationship is unchanged, and the depth must remain unchanged.

Why Axiom D1 is desirable

Depth is meant to quantify centrality relative to the data. Absolute coordinates in \(\mathbb{R}^d\) have no intrinsic statistical meaning. Without D1:

  • The choice of origin would matter
  • Identical data configurations at different locations would yield different depths
  • The notion of a ‚Äúcenter‚Äù would be ill-defined

Verification for the \(L_2\)-depth

Step 1: Definition

The \(L_2\)-depth of \(z \in \mathbb{R}^d\) with respect to \(X \sim P\) is defined as

\[ D^{L_2}(z \mid X) = \bigl(1 + \mathbb{E}|z - X|\bigr)^{-1}. \]

Step 2: Translate data and point

Fix an arbitrary translation vector \(b \in \mathbb{R}^d\). Define

  • Translated point: \(z' = z + b\)
  • Translated random vector: \(X' = X + b\)

We compare \(D^{L_2}(z \mid X)\) and \(D^{L_2}(z' \mid X')\).

Step 3: Expand the translated depth

By definition,

\[ D^{L_2}(z' \mid X') = \bigl(1 + \mathbb{E}|z' - X'|\bigr)^{-1}. \]

Substituting \(z' = z + b\) and \(X' = X + b\),

\[ |z' - X'| = |(z + b) - (X + b)|. \]

Step 4: Use translation invariance of subtraction

For all \(a,b,c \in \mathbb{R}^d\),

\[ (a + c) - (b + c) = a - b. \]

Hence, pointwise,

\[ |(z + b) - (X + b)| = |z - X|. \]

Step 5: Take expectations

Since the integrands are identical almost surely,

\[ \mathbb{E}|z' - X'| = \mathbb{E}|z - X|. \]

Step 6: Conclude equality of depths

Substituting back,

\[ D^{L_2}(z' \mid X') = \bigl(1 + \mathbb{E}|z - X|\bigr)^{-1} = D^{L_2}(z \mid X). \]

Conclusion

The \(L_2\)-depth satisfies Axiom D1 (Translation Invariance):

\[ \boxed{ D^{L_2}(z \mid X) = D^{L_2}(z + b \mid X + b) \quad \forall z,b \in \mathbb{R}^d } \]

Geometric intuition

The \(L_2\)-depth measures average distance from the data cloud. Shifting all points by the same vector does not change any distances. The definition depends only on differences \(z - X\), and differences are blind to absolute location.

This reasoning holds in one dimension, two dimensions, and in higher dimensions. Translation invariance is therefore not an added feature but a structural consequence of how \(L_2\)-depth is defined.

\(L_2\)-Depth and Axiom D2 (Linear Invariance)

Axiom D2: Formal statement

Let \(X\) be an \(\mathbb{R}^d\)-valued random vector with distribution \(P\). Let \(D(\cdot \mid X)\) be a statistical depth function.

Axiom D2 (Linear Invariance). For every \(z \in \mathbb{R}^d\) and every invertible matrix \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{d\times d}\),

\[ D(z \mid X) = D(Az \mid AX), \]

where \(AX\) denotes the random vector obtained by applying the linear map \(A\) to \(X\).

Meaning of Axiom D2

Linear invariance requires depth to be unaffected by any invertible change of coordinates, including rotations, reflections, scalings, and shear transformations. Depth should depend on the intrinsic geometry of the data configuration, not on the coordinate system used to describe it.

Why Axiom D2 is desirable

Without D2:

  • The same data cloud viewed in different coordinate systems may yield different depths
  • Centrality would depend on units of measurement or axis orientation
  • Comparisons across transformed datasets would be meaningless

Combined with D1, linear invariance yields affine invariance, ensuring that depth is a geometric property of the distribution rather than of its representation.

Verification for the \(L_2\)-depth

Step 1: Definition

The \(L_2\)-depth is defined as

\[ D^{L_2}(z \mid X) = \bigl(1 + \mathbb{E}|z - X|\bigr)^{-1}. \]

Step 2: Apply a general linear transformation

Let \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{d\times d}\) be invertible. Define

  • Transformed point: \(z' = Az\)
  • Transformed random vector: \(X' = AX\)

We compare \(D^{L_2}(z \mid X)\) and \(D^{L_2}(z' \mid X')\).

Step 3: Expand the transformed depth

By definition,

\[ D^{L_2}(z' \mid X') = \bigl(1 + \mathbb{E}|Az - AX|\bigr)^{-1}. \]

Using linearity,

\[ Az - AX = A(z - X), \qquad |Az - AX| = |A(z - X)|. \]

Step 4: Use properties of the Euclidean norm

The Euclidean norm satisfies

\[ |Av| = |v| \quad \text{for all } v \in \mathbb{R}^d \]

if and only if \(A\) is orthogonal. For a general invertible matrix, there exist constants

\[ 0 < c_A \le C_A < \infty \]

such that

\[ c_A |v| \le |Av| \le C_A |v| \quad \text{for all } v \in \mathbb{R}^d. \]

In general, therefore,

\[ |A(z - X)| \neq |z - X|. \]

Step 5: Compare expectations

Since the integrands differ,

\[ \mathbb{E}|Az - AX| \neq \mathbb{E}|z - X| \quad \text{in general}. \]

Hence,

\[ D^{L_2}(Az \mid AX) = \bigl(1 + \mathbb{E}|A(z - X)|\bigr)^{-1} \neq \bigl(1 + \mathbb{E}|z - X|\bigr)^{-1} = D^{L_2}(z \mid X). \]

Step 6: Explicit one-dimensional counterexample

Let \(d=1\), \(X \sim N(0,1)\), and \(z=0\). Then

\[ D^{L_2}(0 \mid X) = \bigl(1 + \mathbb{E}|X|\bigr)^{-1}. \]

Take the linear transformation \(A = [2]\), so \(AX = 2X\). Then

\[ D^{L_2}(0 \mid 2X) = \bigl(1 + \mathbb{E}|2X|\bigr)^{-1} = \bigl(1 + 2\mathbb{E}|X|\bigr)^{-1} \neq D^{L_2}(0 \mid X). \]

Conclusion

The \(L_2\)-depth does not satisfy Axiom D2 (Linear Invariance):

\[ \boxed{ D^{L_2}(Az \mid AX) \neq D^{L_2}(z \mid X) \quad \text{for general invertible } A } \]

It is invariant only under orthogonal transformations, which is strictly weaker than full linear invariance.

Geometric intuition

The \(L_2\)-depth measures average Euclidean distance. General linear transformations distort distances by stretching different directions unequally. As a result, points that were equally distant before transformation need not remain so afterward.

This behavior reflects that \(L_2\)-depth is fundamentally metric-based rather than affine-geometric.

\(L_2\)-Depth and Axiom D3 (Null at Infinity)

Axiom D3: Formal statement

Let \(X\) be an \(\mathbb{R}^d\)-valued random vector with distribution \(P\). A depth function \(D(\cdot \mid X)\) satisfies Axiom D3 (Null at Infinity) if

\[ |z| \longrightarrow \infty \quad \Longrightarrow \quad D(z \mid X) \longrightarrow 0. \]

Equivalently, for every sequence \((z_n) \subset \mathbb{R}^d\) such that \(|z_n| \to \infty\),

\[ \lim_{n\to\infty} D(z_n \mid X) = 0. \]

Meaning of Axiom D3

Null at infinity requires that points arbitrarily far away from the data cloud must have negligible depth. No point at infinity can be regarded as central. Depth must decay as one moves away from the bulk of the distribution.

Why Axiom D3 is desirable

Without D3:

  • Remote points could retain nontrivial depth
  • Central regions might be unbounded
  • Depth would fail to encode outlyingness

Statistically, D3 enforces a strict separation between center and extremes.

Verification for the \(L_2\)-depth

Step 1: Definition

The \(L_2\)-depth of \(z \in \mathbb{R}^d\) with respect to \(X\) is

\[ D^{L_2}(z \mid X) = \bigl(1 + \mathbb{E}|z - X|\bigr)^{-1}. \]

Assume \(\mathbb{E}|X| < \infty\), which is required for the definition to be well defined.

Step 2: Lower bound on \(\mathbb{E}|z - X|\)

By the reverse triangle inequality, for all \(a,b \in \mathbb{R}^d\),

\[ |a-b| \ge \bigl||a| - |b|\bigr|. \]

Applying this with \(a=z\) and \(b=X\),

\[ |z - X| \ge |z| - |X|. \]

This inequality holds pointwise and hence almost surely.

Step 3: Take expectations

Taking expectations yields

\[ \mathbb{E}|z - X| \ge \mathbb{E}(|z| - |X|) = |z| - \mathbb{E}|X|. \]

Step 4: Let \(|z| \to \infty\)

Since \(\mathbb{E}|X| < \infty\) is fixed,

\[ |z| - \mathbb{E}|X| \longrightarrow \infty \quad \text{as } |z| \to \infty. \]

Hence,

\[ \mathbb{E}|z - X| \longrightarrow \infty. \]

Step 5: Conclude decay of depth

Recalling

\[ D^{L_2}(z \mid X) = \frac{1}{1 + \mathbb{E}|z - X|}, \]

divergence of the denominator implies

\[ |z| \to \infty \quad \Longrightarrow \quad D^{L_2}(z \mid X) \to 0. \]

Conclusion

The \(L_2\)-depth satisfies Axiom D3 (Null at Infinity):

\[ \boxed{ |z| \to \infty \;\Longrightarrow\; D^{L_2}(z \mid X) \to 0 } \]

Geometric intuition

The \(L_2\)-depth is the reciprocal of one plus the average distance from the data cloud. As \(z\) moves farther away, all observations become increasingly distant. Even the closest data points cannot offset this growing baseline distance.

This decay occurs in one dimension, in the plane, and in higher dimensions alike. Depth therefore fades smoothly and inevitably at infinity.

\(L_2\)-Depth and Axiom D4 (Monotonicity on Rays)

Axiom D4: Formal statement

Let \(D(\cdot \mid X)\) be a depth function on \(\mathbb{R}^d\). Let \(z^\ast \in \mathbb{R}^d\) be a deepest point, that is,

\[ D(z^\ast \mid X) = \sup_{z \in \mathbb{R}^d} D(z \mid X). \]

Axiom D4 (Monotonicity on Rays). For every \(z \in \mathbb{R}^d\) and every \(t \in [0,1]\),

\[ D\bigl(z^\ast + t(z - z^\ast) \mid X\bigr) \;\ge\; D(z \mid X). \]

Equivalently, along any ray emanating from a deepest point, depth does not increase as one moves away from that deepest point.

Meaning of Axiom D4

Starting from a deepest point and moving in any direction, depth should decrease monotonically, or at least not increase. There should be no secondary peaks of depth farther away from the center.

Why Axiom D4 is desirable

Without D4:

  • Central regions could be disconnected or shell-like
  • A point farther from the center could appear more central than a closer one
  • Depth contours would lose geometric meaning

Axiom D4 enforces a single-center geometry: depth behaves like a hill with no bumps.

Verification for the \(L_2\)-depth

Step 1: Definition and deepest points

The \(L_2\)-depth is defined by

\[ D^{L_2}(z \mid X) = \bigl(1 + \mathbb{E}|z - X|\bigr)^{-1}. \]

Depth is maximized when \(\mathbb{E}|z - X|\) is minimized. Hence a deepest point \(z^\ast\) is any spatial median:

\[ z^\ast \in \arg\min_{z \in \mathbb{R}^d} \mathbb{E}|z - X|. \]

Step 2: Reduction to an inequality for expectations

The mapping \(u \mapsto (1+u)^{-1}\) is strictly decreasing on \([0,\infty)\). Therefore, Axiom D4 is equivalent to showing that, for all \(z \in \mathbb{R}^d\) and \(t \in [0,1]\),

\[ \mathbb{E}|z^\ast + t(z - z^\ast) - X| \;\le\; \mathbb{E}|z - X|. \]

Step 3: Convexity of the Euclidean norm

For every fixed \(x \in \mathbb{R}^d\), the function

\[ f_x(z) = |z - x| \]

is convex on \(\mathbb{R}^d\). Hence, for all \(z_1, z_2 \in \mathbb{R}^d\) and \(t \in [0,1]\),

\[ |t z_1 + (1-t) z_2 - x| \;\le\; t|z_1 - x| + (1-t)|z_2 - x|. \]

Step 4: Apply convexity pointwise

Fix a realization \(X = x\) and apply the inequality with

\[ z_1 = z, \qquad z_2 = z^\ast. \]

This yields

\[ |z^\ast + t(z - z^\ast) - x| \;\le\; t|z - x| + (1-t)|z^\ast - x|. \]

Step 5: Take expectations

Taking expectations with respect to \(X\),

\[ \mathbb{E}|z^\ast + t(z - z^\ast) - X| \;\le\; t\,\mathbb{E}|z - X| + (1-t)\,\mathbb{E}|z^\ast - X|. \]

Step 6: Use minimality of the spatial median

By definition of \(z^\ast\),

\[ \mathbb{E}|z^\ast - X| \;\le\; \mathbb{E}|z - X| \quad \text{for all } z \in \mathbb{R}^d. \]

Substituting,

\[ \mathbb{E}|z^\ast + t(z - z^\ast) - X| \;\le\; \mathbb{E}|z - X|. \]

Step 7: Translate back to depth

Since depth is a decreasing function of the expectation,

\[ D^{L_2}(z^\ast + t(z - z^\ast) \mid X) \;\ge\; D^{L_2}(z \mid X). \]

Conclusion

The \(L_2\)-depth satisfies Axiom D4 (Monotonicity on Rays):

\[ \boxed{ D^{L_2}(z^\ast + t(z - z^\ast) \mid X) \;\ge\; D^{L_2}(z \mid X) \quad \forall\, t \in [0,1] } \]

Geometric intuition

The \(L_2\)-depth is based on a convex objective: average distance. Convexity implies the absence of secondary minima and enforces monotone behavior along straight paths toward the minimizer.

In one dimension, distance is V-shaped around the median. In higher dimensions, level sets remain convex, and any straight path toward the center moves inward. This is why central regions of \(L_2\)-depth are not merely star-shaped but genuinely convex.

\(L_2\)-Depth and Axiom D5 (Upper Semicontinuity)

Axiom D5: Formal statement

Let \(D(\cdot \mid X)\) be a depth function on \(\mathbb{R}^d\).

Axiom D5 (Upper semicontinuity). For every \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}\), the upper level set

\[ D_\alpha(X) := \{ z \in \mathbb{R}^d : D(z \mid X) \ge \alpha \} \]

is a closed subset of \(\mathbb{R}^d\).

Equivalently,

\[ \limsup_{n\to\infty} D(z_n \mid X) \le D(z \mid X) \quad \text{whenever } z_n \to z . \]

Meaning of Axiom D5

Upper semicontinuity prevents depth from jumping upward at a limit point. If a sequence of points has depth at least \(\alpha\), then its limit must also have depth at least \(\alpha\).

Why Axiom D5 is desirable

  • Ensures central regions are closed
  • Guarantees existence of deepest points
  • Prevents pathological ‚Äúlimit-only‚Äù medians

Axiom D5 guarantees geometric regularity and well-posedness of depth-based medians.

Verification for the \(L_2\)-depth

Step 1: Definition

The \(L_2\)-depth is

\[ D^{L_2}(z \mid X) = \bigl(1 + \mathbb{E}|z - X|\bigr)^{-1}. \]

For any \(\alpha \in (0,1]\),

\[ D^{L_2}(z \mid X) \ge \alpha \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \mathbb{E}|z - X| \le \frac{1}{\alpha} - 1. \]

Hence the upper level set is

\[ D^{L_2}_\alpha(X) = \left\{ z \in \mathbb{R}^d : \mathbb{E}|z - X| \le c_\alpha \right\}, \qquad c_\alpha := \frac{1}{\alpha} - 1. \]

Step 2: Reduction

It suffices to show that the function

\[ f(z) := \mathbb{E}|z - X| \]

is continuous on \(\mathbb{R}^d\), since sublevel sets of continuous functions are closed.

Step 3: Pointwise convergence

Let \(z_n \to z\). For each fixed realization \(X=x\),

\[ |z_n - x| \longrightarrow |z - x| \]

by continuity of the Euclidean norm. Thus \(|z_n - X| \to |z - X|\) almost surely.

Step 4: Dominated convergence

For all \(n\),

\[ |z_n - X| \le |z_n| + |X|. \]

Since \(z_n \to z\), the sequence \(|z_n|\) is bounded, so there exists \(M<\infty\) with

\[ |z_n - X| \le M + |X|. \]

Assuming \(\mathbb{E}|X| < \infty\), the dominating function is integrable. By the Dominated Convergence Theorem,

\[ \mathbb{E}|z_n - X| \longrightarrow \mathbb{E}|z - X|. \]

Step 5: Closedness of upper level sets

Since \(f(z)\) is continuous,

\[ D^{L_2}_\alpha(X) = f^{-1}((-\infty,c_\alpha]) \]

is closed. Therefore, \(D^{L_2}(\cdot \mid X)\) is upper semicontinuous.

Conclusion

The \(L_2\)-depth satisfies Axiom D5 (Upper semicontinuity):

\[ \boxed{ D^{L_2}(\cdot \mid X) \text{ is upper semicontinuous on } \mathbb{R}^d } \]

Geometric intuition

Average distance varies smoothly with location. There is no mechanism by which an infinitesimal movement can suddenly increase centrality. As a result, depth contours form smooth, closed shells shrinking continuously toward the spatial median.

This guarantees that deepest points genuinely exist and are not merely limiting artifacts.

Affine-Invariant \(L_2\)-Depth

Depth under study

Let \(X \sim P\) be an \(\mathbb{R}^d\)-valued random vector. Let \(S_X \in \mathbb{R}^{d\times d}\) be a positive definite, affine-equivariant scatter functional, satisfying

\[ S_{AX+b} = A S_X A^\top \quad \text{for all invertible } A \text{ and all } b \in \mathbb{R}^d. \]

Define the Mahalanobis-type norm

\[ |u|_{S_X} := \sqrt{u^\top S_X^{-1} u}. \]

The affine-invariant \(L_2\)-depth is

\[ D^{L_2}_{\mathrm{aff}}(z \mid X) = \bigl(1 + \mathbb{E}|z - X|_{S_X}\bigr)^{-1}. \]

Assume throughout that \(S_X\) is positive definite and \(\mathbb{E}|X|_{S_X} < \infty\).

Axiom D1 ‚Äî Translation invariance

For all \(z,b \in \mathbb{R}^d\),

\[ D(z \mid X) = D(z+b \mid X+b). \]

Proof

Let \(X' = X + b\) and \(z' = z + b\). By affine equivariance,

\[ S_{X'} = S_{X+b} = S_X. \]

Therefore,

\[ |z' - X'|_{S_{X'}} = |(z+b)-(X+b)|_{S_X} = |z - X|_{S_X}. \]

Taking expectations yields

\[ D^{L_2}_{\mathrm{aff}}(z+b \mid X+b) = D^{L_2}_{\mathrm{aff}}(z \mid X). \]

Conclusion: affine-invariant \(L_2\)-depth satisfies D1.

Axiom D2 ‚Äî Linear (Affine) invariance

For every invertible \(A\),

\[ D(z \mid X) = D(Az \mid AX). \]

Proof

Let \(X' = AX\) and \(z' = Az\). By affine equivariance,

\[ S_{X'} = S_{AX} = A S_X A^\top. \]

Then

\[ |z' - X'|_{S_{X'}} = \sqrt{(Az-AX)^\top (A S_X A^\top)^{-1} (Az-AX)}. \]

Using \((A S_X A^\top)^{-1} = A^{-\top} S_X^{-1} A^{-1}\), we obtain

\[ |z' - X'|_{S_{X'}} = \sqrt{(z-X)^\top S_X^{-1} (z-X)} = |z - X|_{S_X}. \]

Hence expectations coincide and

\[ D^{L_2}_{\mathrm{aff}}(Az \mid AX) = D^{L_2}_{\mathrm{aff}}(z \mid X). \]

Conclusion: affine-invariant \(L_2\)-depth satisfies D2.

Axiom D3 ‚Äî Null at infinity

By the reverse triangle inequality in Mahalanobis norm,

\[ |z-X|_{S_X} \ge |z|_{S_X} - |X|_{S_X}. \]

Taking expectations gives

\[ \mathbb{E}|z-X|_{S_X} \ge |z|_{S_X} - \mathbb{E}|X|_{S_X}. \]

Since \(|z|_{S_X} \to \infty\) as \(|z| \to \infty\), it follows that

\[ D^{L_2}_{\mathrm{aff}}(z \mid X) \to 0. \]

Conclusion: affine-invariant \(L_2\)-depth satisfies D3.

Axiom D4 ‚Äî Monotonicity on rays

The proof mirrors the \(L_2\)-depth case:

  • \(z^\ast\) minimizes \(\mathbb{E}|z-X|_{S_X}\)
  • \(|z-X|_{S_X}\) is convex in \(z\)
  • Expectation preserves convexity

Hence, for all \(t \in [0,1]\),

\[ D^{L_2}_{\mathrm{aff}}(z^\ast + t(z-z^\ast)) \ge D^{L_2}_{\mathrm{aff}}(z). \]

Conclusion: affine-invariant \(L_2\)-depth satisfies D4.

Axiom D5 ‚Äî Upper semicontinuity

The map \(z \mapsto |z-X|_{S_X}\) is continuous. Under the finite first-moment assumption, dominated convergence implies continuity of \(\mathbb{E}|z-X|_{S_X}\). Therefore, upper level sets are closed.

Conclusion: affine-invariant \(L_2\)-depth satisfies D5.

Mahalanobis Depth

Depth under study

Let \(X \sim P\) be an \(\mathbb{R}^d\)-valued random vector. Let

  • \(c_X \in \mathbb{R}^d\) be an affine-equivariant location functional, satisfying

    \[ c_{AX+b} = A c_X + b \quad \text{for all invertible } A \text{ and } b \in \mathbb{R}^d, \]

  • \(S_X \in \mathbb{R}^{d\times d}\) be a positive definite affine-equivariant scatter functional, satisfying

    \[ S_{AX+b} = A S_X A^\top. \]

The Mahalanobis depth is defined as

\[ D^{\mathrm{Mah}}(z \mid X) = \bigl(1 + (z - c_X)^\top S_X^{-1} (z - c_X)\bigr)^{-1}. \]

Assume throughout that \(S_X\) is positive definite.

Axiom D1 ‚Äî Translation invariance

For all \(z,b \in \mathbb{R}^d\),

\[ D(z \mid X) = D(z+b \mid X+b). \]

Let \(X' = X + b\) and \(z' = z + b\). By affine equivariance,

\[ c_{X'} = c_X + b, \qquad S_{X'} = S_X. \]

Then

\[ z' - c_{X'} = z - c_X, \]

and hence

\[ (z' - c_{X'})^\top S_{X'}^{-1} (z' - c_{X'}) = (z-c_X)^\top S_X^{-1} (z-c_X). \]

Substitution yields

\[ D^{\mathrm{Mah}}(z+b \mid X+b) = D^{\mathrm{Mah}}(z \mid X). \]

Conclusion: Mahalanobis depth satisfies D1.

Axiom D2 ‚Äî Linear (Affine) invariance

For all invertible \(A\),

\[ D(z \mid X) = D(Az \mid AX). \]

Let \(X' = AX\) and \(z' = Az\). By affine equivariance,

\[ c_{X'} = A c_X, \qquad S_{X'} = A S_X A^\top. \]

Then

\[ z' - c_{X'} = A(z-c_X). \]

Using \((A S_X A^\top)^{-1} = A^{-\top} S_X^{-1} A^{-1}\), we obtain

\[ (z' - c_{X'})^\top S_{X'}^{-1} (z' - c_{X'}) = (z-c_X)^\top S_X^{-1} (z-c_X). \]

Thus

\[ D^{\mathrm{Mah}}(Az \mid AX) = D^{\mathrm{Mah}}(z \mid X). \]

Conclusion: Mahalanobis depth satisfies D2.

Axiom D3 ‚Äî Null at infinity

Since \(S_X^{-1}\) is positive definite, there exists \(\lambda_{\min} > 0\) such that

\[ (z-c_X)^\top S_X^{-1} (z-c_X) \ge \lambda_{\min} |z-c_X|^2. \]

As \(|z| \to \infty\), also \(|z-c_X| \to \infty\), hence the quadratic form diverges. Therefore,

\[ D^{\mathrm{Mah}}(z \mid X) \to 0. \]

Conclusion: Mahalanobis depth satisfies D3.

Axiom D4 ‚Äî Monotonicity on rays

The depth is maximized when the quadratic form is minimized. The unique minimizer is

\[ z^\ast = c_X. \]

Let \(z_t = c_X + t(z-c_X)\) for \(t \in [0,1]\). Then

\[ (z_t-c_X)^\top S_X^{-1}(z_t-c_X) = t^2 (z-c_X)^\top S_X^{-1}(z-c_X). \]

Since \(t^2 \le 1\), the quadratic form decreases along the ray toward \(c_X\). As depth is a decreasing function of this form,

\[ D^{\mathrm{Mah}}(z_t \mid X) \ge D^{\mathrm{Mah}}(z \mid X). \]

Conclusion: Mahalanobis depth satisfies D4.

Axiom D5 ‚Äî Upper semicontinuity

The mapping

\[ z \mapsto (z-c_X)^\top S_X^{-1}(z-c_X) \]

is a continuous polynomial function. Hence

\[ D^{\mathrm{Mah}}(z \mid X) = \bigl(1+\text{quadratic form}\bigr)^{-1} \]

is continuous, and its upper level sets are closed.

Conclusion: Mahalanobis depth satisfies D5.

Projection Depth

Depth under study

Let \(X \sim P\) be an \(\mathbb{R}^d\)-valued random vector, and let

\[ S^{d-1}=\{p\in\mathbb{R}^d:\ |p|=1\}. \]

For a real-valued random variable \(U\):

  • \(\operatorname{med}(U)\) denotes a median
  • \(\operatorname{Dmed}(U):=\operatorname{med}(|U-\operatorname{med}(U)|)\) denotes the MAD

Assume \(\operatorname{Dmed}(\langle p,X\rangle)>0\) for all \(p\in S^{d-1}\).

The projection depth is defined by

\[ D^{\mathrm{proj}}(z\mid X) = \Bigl(1+\sup_{p\in S^{d-1}} \frac{\bigl|\langle p,z\rangle-\operatorname{med}(\langle p,X\rangle)\bigr|} {\operatorname{Dmed}(\langle p,X\rangle)} \Bigr)^{-1}. \]

Axiom D1 ‚Äî Translation invariance

Let \(b\in\mathbb{R}^d\) and define \(z'=z+b\), \(X'=X+b\). For any \(p\in S^{d-1}\),

\[ \langle p,z'\rangle-\operatorname{med}(\langle p,X'\rangle) = \langle p,z\rangle-\operatorname{med}(\langle p,X\rangle). \]

Moreover, \(\operatorname{Dmed}(\langle p,X'\rangle)=\operatorname{Dmed}(\langle p,X\rangle)\). Hence the standardized deviations coincide for all \(p\), so

\[ D^{\mathrm{proj}}(z+b\mid X+b)=D^{\mathrm{proj}}(z\mid X). \]

Conclusion: projection depth satisfies D1.

Axiom D2 ‚Äî Linear (Affine) invariance

Let \(A\) be invertible and define \(z'=Az\), \(X'=AX\). For \(p\in S^{d-1}\),

\[ \langle p,Az\rangle=\langle A^\top p,z\rangle. \]

Define \(q:=\frac{A^\top p}{|A^\top p|}\in S^{d-1}\). Then

\[ \langle p,Az\rangle=|A^\top p|\langle q,z\rangle, \qquad \langle p,AX\rangle=|A^\top p|\langle q,X\rangle. \]

Using the scaling properties of the median and MAD,

\[ \frac{|\langle p,Az\rangle-\operatorname{med}(\langle p,AX\rangle)|} {\operatorname{Dmed}(\langle p,AX\rangle)} = \frac{|\langle q,z\rangle-\operatorname{med}(\langle q,X\rangle)|} {\operatorname{Dmed}(\langle q,X\rangle)}. \]

As \(p\) ranges over \(S^{d-1}\), so does \(q\). Therefore,

\[ D^{\mathrm{proj}}(Az\mid AX)=D^{\mathrm{proj}}(z\mid X). \]

Conclusion: projection depth satisfies D2.

Axiom D3 ‚Äî Null at infinity

Fix any \(p\in S^{d-1}\). As \(|z|\to\infty\), we have \(|\langle p,z\rangle|\to\infty\). Since \(\operatorname{med}(\langle p,X\rangle)\) and \(\operatorname{Dmed}(\langle p,X\rangle)\) are finite and positive, the standardized deviation diverges, and hence

\[ D^{\mathrm{proj}}(z\mid X)\to 0. \]

Conclusion: projection depth satisfies D3.

Axiom D4 ‚Äî Monotonicity on rays

Define the outlyingness function

\[ O(z):=\sup_{p\in S^{d-1}} \frac{|\langle p,z\rangle-\operatorname{med}(\langle p,X\rangle)|} {\operatorname{Dmed}(\langle p,X\rangle)}. \]

For fixed \(p\), the mapping in \(z\) is convex. The supremum of convex functions is convex, hence \(O(z)\) is convex. If \(z^\ast\) minimizes \(O\), then convexity yields

\[ O(z^\ast+t(z-z^\ast))\le O(z) \quad \forall\, t\in[0,1]. \]

Since \(D^{\mathrm{proj}}=(1+O)^{-1}\) is decreasing in \(O\),

\[ D^{\mathrm{proj}}(z^\ast+t(z-z^\ast)\mid X)\ge D^{\mathrm{proj}}(z\mid X). \]

Conclusion: projection depth satisfies D4.

Axiom D5 ‚Äî Upper semicontinuity

For each fixed \(p\), the standardized deviation is continuous in \(z\). The supremum over \(p\in S^{d-1}\) of continuous functions is upper semicontinuous. Therefore \(O(z)\) is upper semicontinuous, and so is

\[ D^{\mathrm{proj}}(z\mid X)=(1+O(z))^{-1}. \]

Upper level sets are closed.

Conclusion: projection depth satisfies D5.
